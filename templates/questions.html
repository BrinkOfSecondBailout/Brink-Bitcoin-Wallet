What is Bitcoin?

There's probably an infinite amount of ways to describe bitcoin.

But for me, bitcoin is a decentralized, permissioned-less, immutable, digital ledger that keeps track of all transactions within the network.

This allows for individuals to exchange values peer to peer without a middle man, eliminating a multitude of problems that are caused by the traditional, legacy financial system.

Bitcoin also has a strict supply of 21 million coins, enforced by every node and participant in the network, making it the most verifiably scarced asset in the history of humankind.

How does Brink Wallet work?

Your Private Key

When you first sign up to Brink Wallet, we randomly generate a bitcoin private key for you.

This private key is then stored on our database. Hence this is called a HOT wallet, meaning the security is significantly lower than that of a cold wallet.

A cold wallet is one that generates a private key for you and does not store it. It's up to YOU to manage this private key.

If your private key is stored by the custodian, (similar to this wallet), the risk of the custodian being hacked and lose your funds is very high.

Remember, anyone who knows your private key has control of your bitcoin.

In a hot wallet, you sacrifice security for convenience.

This is why it's crucial to remember that Brink is an experimental wallet, made for educational purpose, and to NEVER send any large amount (more than a dollar or two worth) of bitcoin to it. If any.

It is much safer to purchase a cold wallet, generate your private key, then store and protect your own key instead.

I recommend cold wallets such as Jade, Coldcard, or SeedSigner (I am not sponsored by these companies)

Your Public Key

Your public key is derived from your private key cryptographically.

The public key can be revealed with low consequences, unlike the private key.

This public key, however, will then be used to cryptographically generate a public address

Typically, the public key is only revealed when you are sending bitcoin to another party, not the other way around.

The public key will help the other party to verify that it's indeed you who owns the bitcoin being sent.

Your Public Address

The public address is derived cryptographically from the public key.

This is all you need to give out in order to receive bitcoin to your wallet.

Ideally, you should use a freshly generated address per transaction.

It's okay to re-use the same address, but if you're looking for privacy, it's not encouraged.
